About VarroaMate™

VarroaMate is designed to complement and strengthen national and state Varroa management strategies — supporting the Australian beekeeping community in doing their part to protect colonies, reduce mite loads, and maintain healthy apiaries across the country.

Australian beekeepers now face one of their greatest challenges — Varroa is established and spreading, and the decisions made in the next few years will shape the future of beekeeping across the country. VarroaMate's mission is to give every beekeeper the best possible tools, information, and guidance to protect their colonies, manage mite loads effectively, and help save Australia's bees.

VarroaMate is under ongoing development and welcomes your feedback.

🐝 Join Your Local Bee Club

Beekeeping is best done in community. Your local bee club is one of the most valuable resources available — for mentorship, shared experience, and staying up to date with the Varroa situation in your area. If you are not already a member, we strongly encourage you to join a bee club near you.

🔬 Mite Detection Methods ▼

There are three main methods for detecting Varroa mites. VarroaMate records alcohol wash only — it gives the most accurate and consistent mite count and is the gold standard for monitoring and reporting.

🧪 Alcohol Wash Used by VarroaMate

Wash approximately 300 bees (½ cup) in alcohol — kills bees but dislodges and counts mites accurately. The only method accepted for biosecurity reporting in most states. Results are expressed as mites per 300 bees.

▶ Alcohol Wash video — varroa.org.au ▶ Alcohol Wash — Agriculture Vic
🍬 Sugar Shake Accepted for surveillance

Coat bees in icing sugar and shake over a white surface. Non-lethal but less accurate — mites cling to bees and not all are dislodged. Useful for a quick field check but undercounts by up to 40–60% compared to alcohol wash. Not used for VarroaMate counts.

▶ Sugar Shake video — varroa.org.au
🐝 Drone Uncapping Accepted for surveillance

Uncap drone brood and inspect for mites in cells. Mites preferentially reproduce in drone brood so this can detect early infestations, but cannot be expressed as a standardised count per 300 bees. Not used for VarroaMate counts.

▶ Drone Uncapping video — varroa.org.au ▶ All methods — Bee Aware
💡 Why alcohol wash only? Consistent, standardised counts across time and between beekeepers. Sugar shake can undercount by 40–60%. Alcohol wash is what the national varroa program, AHBIC, and state authorities use as the reference method.

Treatment Library

Varroa treatments grouped by mode of action

📖 This library explains how each treatment works — the chemistry, biology, and practical considerations. It is educational, not prescriptive. Always follow registered product labels and your state's current regulatory requirements.
🧪 Organic Acids
Naturally occurring acids — oxalic acid and formic acid — that are lethal to Varroa at concentrations tolerated by adult bees.
Oxalic Acid (OA)
Registered products: OA Vaporisation · OA Dribble · Aluen CAP · Api-Bioxal
▾
The chemistry

Oxalic acid (C₂H₂O₄) is a dicarboxylic acid — it carries two carboxyl groups (–COOH), meaning it can donate two protons (H⁺). This makes it a diprotic acid, unlike formic acid which is monoprotic (one proton). In water it dissociates in two steps, giving it stronger overall acidifying power at equivalent concentrations. Oxalic acid occurs naturally in many plants — rhubarb, spinach, and beeswax all contain it — and has been used in beekeeping for decades. In Europe it has been registered since the 1980s.

How it affects Varroa

The exact mechanism of action is not fully established, but the leading hypothesis is that oxalic acid disrupts pH-dependent processes in Varroa physiology. The mite's haemolymph (internal fluid) is sensitive to pH changes. Direct contact with oxalic acid — either through grooming, physical contact with treated bees, or vapour absorption — causes ionic disruption and cytotoxic effects. Mites exposed to effective concentrations show paralysis and death within hours to days. Oxalic acid does not penetrate sealed wax cappings, which is why it is significantly more effective in broodless colonies where all mites are on adult bees and fully exposed.

How it affects bees

Adult honey bees are substantially more tolerant of oxalic acid than Varroa mites. The mechanisms behind this differential tolerance are not completely understood, but bees appear to have greater capacity to buffer pH changes and to metabolise or excrete small quantities of the acid. At recommended application rates, short-term effects on adult bees are minimal. However, all OA formulations are harder on brood: OA dribble in particular, when applied with sealed brood present, can damage larvae and pupae exposed through wax cell walls. Repeated vapour treatments at short intervals can also cause measurable increases in adult bee mortality if overdosed.

Impact on honey and frames

Oxalic acid is a naturally occurring constituent of honey. Treatment does not meaningfully increase honey OA levels above natural background concentrations at registered application rates. No withholding period from honey is required for OA vaporisation under most registrations. Frames and wax are not damaged by OA treatment.

Three formulations — why different forms?
OA Vaporisation (sublimation)

OA crystals are heated on a wand or electric plate placed at the hive entrance. The acid sublimates — converting directly from solid to vapour — and deposits as fine crystals on all hive surfaces and bee bodies. Bees moving through the hive distribute and ingest crystals, and mites on adult bees are exposed continuously. Highly effective in broodless colonies; repeated applications (typically 3 × weekly) partially compensate for the presence of capped brood. Does not require opening the hive. The vapour is hazardous to human lungs — full respiratory PPE is essential.

OA Dribble

A warm sugar-OA solution is trickled directly onto the bees between frames. Bees spread the treatment during grooming and trophallaxis. Effective and inexpensive. Requires a broodless colony — OA dribble has poor efficacy when sealed brood is present, as mites in capped cells are not exposed. Only one application per treatment course. Higher short-term bee mortality than vaporisation at equivalent concentrations; considered harder on the colony. Suited to winter broodless treatment in colder climates.

Aluen CAP (slow-release OA + lactic acid)

A polymer cap containing a mixture of oxalic acid and lactic acid that releases slowly over 4–6 weeks. Because release is sustained, it can partially penetrate the brood cycle — some efficacy with brood present, though lower than in broodless conditions. Less abrupt bee exposure than dribble or vapour. Suited to treatment outside the broodless window when other options are limited. No heat or mixing required — practical in the field.

Formic Acid
Registered products: Formic PRO (formic acid strips)
▾
The chemistry

Formic acid (HCOOH) is the simplest carboxylic acid — a single carbon with one carboxyl group. It is monoprotic, donating one proton per molecule, compared to oxalic acid's two. It occurs naturally in ant venom (the name derives from formica, Latin for ant) and in small amounts in honey. Formic acid is a volatile liquid that evaporates at room temperature, making it a fumigant — it acts as vapour within the hive atmosphere rather than through direct bee-to-mite contact.

How it affects Varroa

Formic acid vapour penetrates wax cappings — it is one of the few treatments that reaches mites in sealed brood cells. This makes it uniquely effective with brood present. The acid disrupts mite respiratory and metabolic function through pH-dependent mechanisms and direct cytotoxicity. Efficacy is highly temperature-dependent: below approximately 10°C the evaporation rate drops too low for effective vapour concentrations to build in the hive; above 29–30°C bees may abscond or significant queen mortality can occur.

Temperature requirements

The therapeutic window for formic acid is roughly 10–29°C ambient. Within this range, higher temperatures accelerate evaporation and treatment efficacy, but also increase bee and queen exposure risk. At temperatures approaching 30°C, increased queen loss has been documented. At cooler temperatures, the treatment may be completed with insufficient vapour concentration for full efficacy. Spring and autumn are the optimal treatment windows in most Australian climates.

How it affects bees

Formic acid is irritating to bees at treatment concentrations. Increased fanning behaviour and temporary bees clustering at the entrance (bearding) are common. Some queen loss occurs in a proportion of treatments, particularly at higher temperatures. Worker mortality during the 7-day treatment period is typically measurable but manageable at recommended rates. Adult bees are substantially more tolerant than Varroa.

Impact on honey and frames

Formic acid is a natural honey constituent. Treatment at registered rates does not produce honey residue levels above background. Supers may remain on during treatment under most registrations. No wax or frame damage. Note: a slight increase in honey acidity immediately post-treatment is possible; this normalises quickly.

🌿 Essential Oils — Thymol
Plant-derived phenolic compounds that affect mite neurology and respiration.
Thymol
Registered products: Apiguard · ApiLife Var
▾
The chemistry

Thymol (2-isopropyl-5-methylphenol) is a naturally occurring monoterpenoid phenol found in thyme, oregano, and related plants. It is a white crystalline solid at room temperature, highly aromatic, and moderately volatile. As a phenol, it has antiseptic properties and is active against a broad range of microorganisms. In beekeeping it functions primarily as a fumigant — its vapour builds up within the hive and contacts bees and mites.

How it affects Varroa

Thymol acts through multiple mechanisms. It disrupts octopamine signalling — octopamine is a key neurotransmitter in insects and arachnids (including mites) that regulates behaviour and physiology. Thymol also inhibits acetylcholinesterase activity and disrupts the function of the mite's exoskeletal cuticle, reducing waterproofing and increasing desiccation. The vapour is absorbed through the mite's spiracles (respiratory openings), causing neurological disruption. It does not readily penetrate sealed wax cappings, so efficacy is reduced with significant sealed brood.

Temperature requirements

Thymol's vapour pressure is strongly temperature-dependent. The effective therapeutic window is approximately 15–30°C. Below 15°C, evaporation is insufficient to reach lethal concentrations inside the hive. Above 30°C, evaporation accelerates beyond the therapeutic range and bees may abscond or suffer increased mortality. Thymol is unsuitable for winter treatment and problematic during summer heatwaves. Spring and autumn in temperate Australian climates are optimal windows.

How it affects bees

Bees are significantly more tolerant of thymol than mites, but at treatment concentrations the compound is irritating. Increased entrance bearding, reduced foraging, and temporary disruption of colony behaviour are common. Brood mortality can occur at higher temperatures when vapour concentrations spike. Both Apiguard and ApiLife Var require honey super removal, as thymol taints honey at detectable levels.

Impact on honey and frames

Thymol taints honey at detectable and unacceptable levels at treatment concentrations. Honey supers must be removed before treatment. Frames and wax are not chemically damaged, though the hive environment during treatment carries a strong thyme-like odour that dissipates after treatment completion. A withholding period before honey production should be observed.

⚗️ Synthetic Acaricides
Synthetic compounds targeting the mite nervous system. Highly effective but resistance risk increases with repeated use of the same mode of action.
Amitraz — Formamidines
Registered products: Apivar · Apitraz
▾
The chemistry

Amitraz is a member of the formamidine chemical class — compounds characterised by the –N=CH–N– functional group. It is a synthetic acaricide and insecticide developed in the 1970s and widely used in agriculture for tick and mite control. In beekeeping it is impregnated into plastic strips that are hung between brood frames, releasing the active ingredient slowly over 6–8 weeks as bees rub against them and distribute amitraz through the colony.

How it affects Varroa

Amitraz is an octopamine receptor agonist — it mimics and over-stimulates the octopamine signalling system in the mite's nervous system. Octopamine is the invertebrate equivalent of adrenaline, regulating aggression, locomotion, and many basic physiological functions. Over-stimulation causes hyperactivity, loss of coordination, paralysis, and death. It also inhibits monoamine oxidase (MAO), an enzyme involved in breaking down neurotransmitters, compounding the neurological disruption. Amitraz is highly efficacious — mite mortality begins within hours of exposure. It is active with brood present as mites carry amitraz into cells on their bodies.

Temperature requirements

Amitraz efficacy is temperature-dependent — at temperatures below approximately 10°C, bees cluster and reduce movement, limiting the physical contact mechanism needed to distribute amitraz through the colony. Treatment during extended cold winters in cool-climate apiaries may be less effective. For most of Australia, this is not a major constraint except in alpine zones in mid-winter.

Resistance risk

Amitraz resistance in Varroa has been documented in multiple countries. In 2024–25, NSW DPIRD confirmed the first cases of suspected amitraz resistance in Australian Varroa populations. Resistance develops when colonies are treated repeatedly with the same mode of action — mites with slightly altered octopamine receptors survive, reproduce, and pass on resistance. Rotation with other modes of action (organic acids, essential oils, physical methods) is strongly recommended to slow resistance development.

How it affects bees

At registered rates, amitraz has a good safety margin for adult bees — they are substantially less sensitive to octopamine-pathway disruption than mites. Queen loss and brood effects are rare at recommended application rates. Amitraz has been detected in beeswax and some studies report sub-lethal behavioural effects on worker bees at elevated wax residue levels.

Impact on honey and frames

Amitraz and its breakdown products accumulate in beeswax over repeated treatments. Wax residues persist and may transfer to subsequent crops. Honey supers must be removed during treatment. A withholding period is required before honey production. Long-term wax management — including periodic comb replacement — is important for operations using amitraz regularly.

Pyrethroids
Registered products: Bayvarol (flumethrin) · Apistan (tau-fluvalinate)
▾
The chemistry

Pyrethroids are synthetic analogues of pyrethrin — the natural insecticide produced by chrysanthemum flowers. They are highly lipophilic (fat-soluble) compounds that bind strongly to biological membranes and accumulate in wax. In beekeeping, flumethrin (Bayvarol) and tau-fluvalinate (Apistan) are impregnated into plastic strips placed between brood frames. Their mode of action is identical — contact exposure as bees brush against strips and distribute the compound through the colony.

How it affects Varroa

Pyrethroids are voltage-gated sodium channel modulators. They bind to sodium channels in nerve cell membranes and hold them in an open state, causing continuous nerve firing, paralysis, and death. This is a fundamentally different mode of action from amitraz (octopamine agonism) and organic acids (pH disruption). What makes pyrethroids unusual in beekeeping is that honey bees have a natural metabolic tolerance — they can rapidly detoxify fluvalinate through enzymatic breakdown — while Varroa mites lack this detoxification capacity and are killed at concentrations bees tolerate well.

Resistance — a serious concern

Pyrethroid resistance in Varroa is widespread globally and well-documented in Australia. Resistance arises through mutations in the sodium channel target site (knockdown resistance, kdr) that reduce binding affinity, as well as through upregulation of metabolic detoxification enzymes. In resistant populations, treatment efficacy drops dramatically — field studies have recorded efficacies below 20% in some resistant Australian populations. Varroa populations should be tested for pyrethroid susceptibility before use. NSW DPIRD has confirmed resistance in multiple NSW apiaries. Pyrethroids should not be used as a first-line treatment in areas with known resistance.

How it affects bees

At registered concentrations, adult bees tolerate pyrethroids well due to their detoxification enzymes. However, sub-lethal effects — including impaired navigation, learning, and foraging efficiency — have been reported at elevated wax residue levels. Brood is generally not affected at recommended rates.

Impact on honey and frames

Pyrethroids are highly lipophilic and accumulate extensively in beeswax — wax residues from repeated treatment can reach levels that interfere with brood development in subsequent seasons. Honey supers must be removed during treatment. Wax from heavily treated colonies should be replaced or excluded from cappings wax used in food products. Residues in honey itself are generally low when supers are removed, but monitoring is advisable in high-use operations.

🔧 Physical & Mechanical Methods
Chemical-free approaches that exploit the mite's biology and brood cycle. No resistance risk, but require precise timing and hands-on management.
Drone Brood Removal
Mechanical — exploits Varroa's preference for drone brood
▾
How it works

Varroa mites strongly prefer drone brood for reproduction — studies show mites enter drone cells at 8–10 times the rate of worker cells. This preference arises because drone brood has a longer capping period (24 days vs 12 for workers), giving mites more time to complete their reproductive cycle and produce more offspring per cell. By placing a frame of drone-sized foundation in the brood nest and removing it once capped, beekeepers trap a disproportionate number of reproductive mites and physically remove them from the colony — along with the developing drone brood — by freezing or destroying the frame.

Effectiveness and limitations

Drone brood removal provides meaningful mite suppression during the active season but is rarely sufficient as a standalone treatment in high-pressure situations. It works best as part of an integrated program. Effectiveness depends on drone rearing — it is only applicable during spring and summer when drones are being produced. In autumn drones are expelled and in winter rearing ceases, making this method inapplicable outside the active season.

Impact on mites, bees and honey

No chemical impact on bees, honey, or frames. The colony loses drone-rearing investment (energy, wax, nurse bee time) each cycle, which is a minor cost. No resistance risk — this is a physical removal method with no pharmacological component. Drone populations are temporarily reduced, which may slightly affect queen mating if repeated aggressively during swarm season.

Brood Break — Queen Caging
Mechanical — creates a broodless window to maximise OA efficacy
▾
How it works

Isolating the queen in a cage prevents her from laying eggs. After approximately 25 days (the worker brood capping period plus buffer), all sealed brood has emerged, leaving the colony temporarily broodless. In this window, all mites are on adult bees — fully exposed and accessible. An OA treatment (vaporisation or dribble) applied in this broodless window achieves dramatically higher efficacy than OA applied with brood present, because no mites are hidden in sealed cells. The brood break combined with OA is one of the most effective non-chemical-strip treatment combinations available.

Timing and seasonal restrictions

Best timed in late summer (January–February in Australia) when mite-to-bee ratios are highest and there is still sufficient time for the colony to recover before winter bee rearing begins. Caging the queen in autumn or winter is strongly contraindicated — it disrupts the production of long-lived winter bees, weakening the colony at its most vulnerable point. The technique requires a marked queen for efficient cage placement and release.

Impact on bees and honey

No chemical residues. The 25-day broodless period reduces colony population — emerging bees are not replaced during caging. This temporary reduction can affect foraging capacity. Combined with OA vaporisation, there is no honey residue concern. The technique is best paired with post-treatment feeding (sugar syrup) to support colony recovery.

🌡️ Heat Treatment
Exploits the narrow thermal tolerance difference between Varroa mites and honey bee brood. No chemicals, no residues, no resistance risk.
Heat Treatment
Varroa Controller · Duplex-Framebox Plan
▾
The principle — thermal tolerance windows

Honey bee pupae and Varroa mites have different lethal temperature thresholds. Varroa mites are killed by sustained exposure to temperatures around 40–42°C — a range that honey bee brood can tolerate for limited periods. Sealed worker brood is maintained by nurse bees at approximately 34–36°C; drone brood is slightly cooler. The Varroa Controller device heats individual frames to 42°C for a controlled period, exploiting this narrow thermal window to kill mites while preserving developing bees.

Impact on Varroa mites

At 40–42°C, Varroa mites experience protein denaturation, enzymatic failure, and rapid desiccation. Mite mortality at correctly maintained temperature is very high. Critically, mites in sealed cells on the treated frame are killed along with mites on adult bees that are rehoused after treatment. The heat treatment directly addresses the phoretic (on-bee) and reproductive (in-cell) populations on the treated frames simultaneously.

Impact on worker brood

Worker pupae are significantly more heat-tolerant than mites when exposure time is controlled. At 42°C for the treatment duration used by the Varroa Controller, worker brood survival is high — studies report brood viability above 95% in well-conducted treatments. Duration is critical: prolonged overheating (above 43°C or extended time) increases brood mortality. Temperature monitoring and device calibration are important. Slightly older capped worker brood (closer to emergence) is more heat-tolerant than freshly capped larvae.

Impact on drone brood

Drone brood is more heat-sensitive than worker brood. Drone cells are typically housed at slightly lower temperatures in the hive, and drone pupae have a narrower thermal tolerance window. At the 42°C treatment temperature, a higher proportion of drone brood may not survive compared to worker brood. This is a consideration in colonies where drone production is intentionally managed, but for most Varroa-control purposes the trade-off is acceptable. The Duplex-Framebox plan minimises this by concentrating mites into a small number of frames before heat treatment.

Seasonal considerations

Heat treatment is most practical in spring (Sep–Nov) when the brood nest is small — 2–3 frames per session is manageable. As the colony expands through summer, the number of frames requiring treatment increases, making the standard heat treatment protocol impractical. The Duplex-Framebox plan addresses this by using the queen-caging mechanism to concentrate mite reproduction into just two frames before heat treating, making summer heat treatment feasible without treating the full expanded brood nest.

Impact on honey and frames

No chemical residues whatsoever. Honey, wax, frames, and equipment are unaffected. No resistance risk — mites cannot develop behavioural or physiological resistance to heat. Heat treatment is one of the cleanest options available in terms of product quality and wax contamination. The main costs are equipment investment (Varroa Controller device) and time per treatment session.
