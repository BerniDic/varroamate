The VarroaMate Smart Planner exists for one reason — to help you think ahead, act deliberately, and give your colonies the best possible chance against Varroa.

🗓️

Think and plan ahead

Varroa management cannot be improvised. The treatments that work best require advance planning — the right product at the right time, for the right duration. Build your season before it starts.

🧘

Do not be reactive and panic

Panic treatments — grabbing whatever is available when mite levels spike — are rarely effective and often harmful. A calm, pre-planned approach outperforms reactive intervention every time.

🔍

Recognise valuable season/treatment combinations

Some of the most powerful treatment windows are easy to miss — late summer broodless periods, post-flow windows for oxalic acid, spring drone brood trapping. The planner highlights them before they pass.

🍯

Optimal treatment and flow combinations

Not all treatments are compatible with honey supers. Placing the wrong treatment during a flow risks contaminating your harvest. The planner ensures your treatment windows align with — not against — your nectar flows.

❄️

Ensure your bees are ready for winter

Winter bees must be raised from mite-free brood in late summer. Miss this window and your colony enters winter weakened, short-lived, and vulnerable. The planner keeps this critical deadline front and centre.

💛

Keep your bees happy and alive

Everything in this planner comes back to one thing — thriving, productive colonies. Healthy bees, sustainable beekeeping, and the quiet satisfaction of knowing you gave your hives their best chance.

🛡 Colony Survival & Plan Quality
🧮 How your plan is scored

Your plan is assessed across two areas: Colony Survival Chance (the headline indicator — how effectively your treatments protect the colony) and four plan quality dimensions that measure whether your plan is well-structured.

🟢🟡🔴 Colony Survival Chance
Reflects how effectively your actual treatments protect the colony. Built from three factors:

Efficacy ceiling — the best real-world knockdown each treatment type can achieve: Amitraz ~92%, Organic acids ~88–90%, Physical heat ~80%, Thymol ~72%, Pyrethroids ~60% (resistance risk), Mechanical ~40% (adjunct only).

Plan breadth — using 3+ MoA classes (chemical + mechanical + physical) earns a full-spectrum bonus. Mechanical-only is hard-capped at 42%.

Mite pressure — your latest alcohol wash count raises or lowers the score. A strong multi-MoA plan absorbs most of the pressure penalty; a weak plan does not.

Overdue treatments — planned treatments whose month has passed without being logged in VarroaMate apply a gap penalty. The mite population was growing unchecked during that window.

🚨 If Colony Survival drops below 40%, the overall plan rating is capped — a high score elsewhere cannot mask a colony likely to collapse.
Plan quality dimensions (averaged for overall %)
Penalised for every treatment that falls outside your chosen strategy (e.g. a synthetic in an organic-only plan). 100% = all treatments match your philosophy.
📅 Seasonality
Scores seasonal and biological fit. Thymol in winter scores 0% (needs warmth). OA dribble outside winter scores 50% (most effective when broodless). Mechanical-only plans are penalised at high mite pressure. Overdue skipped treatments are excluded.
🔄 MoA Rotation
Consecutive same-MoA treatments are penalised by resistance risk tier: Synthetics −25pts, Thymol −12pts, Organic acids −5pts. Overlapping same-MoA treatments score a conflict penalty. Physical treatments are exempt — no resistance mechanism.
📆 Season Coverage
Rewards targeting spring, summer, and autumn (33% each). Winter is not scored — colonies have low brood and mite pressure is naturally lower in southern hemisphere. At critical pressure, covering only one season applies a steeper penalty.
🍯 Supers Compatibility
Penalises treatments that require supers off (synthetics, thymol) placed during spring/summer honey flow months (Aug–Jan southern hemisphere). OA treatments (vaporisation/dribble), Aluen CAP, Formic PRO, and physical methods are supers-compatible.
⏱ Treatment lifecycle states
Solid bar — future planned treatment, or confirmed in VarroaMate history.
⚠ Amber dashed — planned month has passed but not logged in VarroaMate. Penalises Colony Survival. Log it in VarroaMate to lock it in.
Grey solid — historical treatment from VarroaMate (read-only).

Drone Brood Removal
Varroa mites prefer drone brood — reproducing 8–10× more successfully in drone cells than worker cells. Placing a drone-sized frame then removing it while fully capped traps large numbers of mites with zero chemicals. Best used as a suppression tool in spring alongside a full treatment plan.
⚠️ Timing is everything. Remove the frame too early (before day 20) and mites haven't fully entered the cells — they escape back into the colony. Remove too late (day 25+) and drones begin emerging, releasing all trapped mites back into the hive. The net result is a worse outcome than doing nothing — you've just disrupted the colony with no benefit. Stay in the days 20–24 window.

🖼 Drone frame options — choose what suits your setup
① Plastic drone frame (green)
Entire frame is drone-sized cells. Queen fills readily. Freeze to kill mites, scratch caps, return for bees to clean. Reusable indefinitely. Best for beginners.
② Half-foundation frame
Worker foundation top half only. Bees fill the bottom with natural drone-size comb. No special equipment — any standard deep frame works.
③ Ideal frame in full-depth box
Shorter (ideal/super depth) frame in a full-depth slot. Bees extend drone comb below the frame bottom. Cut out and freeze. No foundation needed.

Brood Break + OA Treatment
Caging the queen for 14 days stops new eggs. Existing brood hatches progressively, creating a window where all mites are phoretic — on adult bees, not in cells. This window is ideal for OA treatment — OA vaporisation (×3 applications), OA dribble (single application, broodless is ideal), or Aluen CAP — all reach maximum efficacy when all mites are phoretic. Use whichever method is registered in your state. The timeline shows all brood cohorts aging through to the broodless window.
⚠️ Precise timing is critical. If you treat before all brood has hatched, mites inside capped cells are completely protected from OA — they only emerge days later to reinfest the colony. Treating even a few days early can leave 30–50% of mites untouched. Use a sticky board from day 18 to confirm broodlessness before applying OA.

Duplex-Framebox Plan
A 24-day integrated system for mid-summer, timed to coincide with honey harvest. The queen is caged in a 2-frame box. Mites funnel into those 2 frames — the only available brood. At day 24 all frames are heat-treated, honey is harvested, and the colony is left broodless for a final OA treatment before winter bee rearing.
⚠️ Day 12 must not slip. The whole system depends on returning to the hive exactly on day 12 while the first frame is fully capped but before any cells begin to open. If you miss this window the mite funnel effect breaks down — drones or bees emerging inside the box release mites back into the brood nest before you can treat them. Mark both dates (Day 12 and Day 24) in your calendar the moment you start.
Days 0–12: Round 1
Brood outside hatches; mites flood into caged frames
Day 12: Extraction
Remove capped frame, heat-treat it, replace with empty frame
Days 12–24: Round 2
All remaining mites funnel into just 2 frames
Day 24: Harvest
Heat-treat both frames, OA treat adults, release queen, feed

The Duplex-Framebox is a ventilated split box placed on top of the brood chamber. The queen is confined inside with 1–2 brood frames and a foundation frame. Worker bees pass freely through the mesh floor, maintaining colony warmth and food supply while the queen's brood is isolated for mite trapping.

Split Brood Break
Walk-away split — divide the colony in two. The queenright half carries on; the queenless half raises a new queen from existing young larvae. The queenless half has a natural brood break lasting ~24 days (no new eggs until the new queen mates and begins laying). This window is ideal for OA treatment and drone frame insertion. Both halves benefit from reduced mite population growth during the split.
Artificial swarm (Pagden method) — move the original hive (with the queen and all bees) to a new location. Put a new box with frames on the original stand. Flying bees return to the original stand, boosting the queenright half. The split on the old stand raises a new queen from open brood frames left behind. The queenless split has a ~24 day brood break — ideal for OA treatment. The Pagden method is the most effective swarm-prevention split and gives you a strong queenright colony for the honey flow.
🧬 Varroa control
Brood break in queenless half stops mite reproduction ~24 days. OA at peak efficacy.
🐝 Colony increase
You end up with two colonies and a fresh, young, mated queen heading the split.
🚫 Swarm prevention
Relieves congestion. Artificial swarm (Pagden) is the gold standard for swarm prevention.
🎯 Drone frame bonus
Insert a drone frame into the queenless half — mites concentrate there with no queen competition. Remove at day 21.

🌡️ Heat Treatment — Varroa Controller
Heat treatment uses the Varroa Controller to heat individual brood frames to 42°C for two hours. At this temperature, Varroa mites die while bee brood survives unharmed. No chemicals, no withholding periods, safe during honey flows.
How it works
  • Remove all capped brood frames from the hive
  • Place frames in the Varroa Controller device
  • Heat to exactly 42°C for 120 minutes — device controls this automatically
  • Return frames to hive — bees clean up dead mites naturally
  • Monitor mite load as usual (alcohol wash) and repeat treatments as long as brood frames are available and mite pressure warrants it — there is no fixed interval
🌱 Easiest in early spring. When the brood nest is at its smallest — typically September — fewer frames need treating per session and the colony is least disrupted. Starting heat treatment early in the season keeps mite populations suppressed before the spring build-up accelerates reproduction.
🔗 Most effective combined with Duplex-Framebox in summer. The Duplex-Framebox concentrates all brood into two frames, making heat treatment dramatically more efficient — instead of treating 8–10 frames across the whole nest you treat just the two loaded frames. This combination is the most thorough chemical-free approach available and is best timed around the summer honey harvest.
⚠️ Temperature matters. Below 42°C mites survive. Above 44°C brood is damaged. The Varroa Controller manages this automatically — do not use improvised heating.
