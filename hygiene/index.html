Why Hygiene Matters More Than Ever
A new era for Australian beekeeping — and why this module exists
🔴 The Varroa Turning Point
Varroa destructor arrived in Australia in 2022 and is now established. This single event has fundamentally changed the disease ecology of Australian honey bee colonies — and with it, the hygiene requirements every beekeeper must meet.

Before Varroa, most bee diseases spread relatively slowly — primarily through contaminated equipment and beekeeper movement. The main threats (AFB, Chalkbrood) were serious but containable with good equipment hygiene.

Varroa changes this completely. It acts as a biological amplifier — vectorising viruses directly into bee haemolymph, enabling viral titres to increase millions-fold in collapsing colonies. A single dying colony can distribute hundreds of mites to neighbours through robbing, triggering cascading losses across an entire apiary.
🌿 Beyond Varroa — Compounding Stressors
Varroa does not operate in isolation. Australian beekeepers now face a convergence of stressors that collectively reduce colony resilience and amplify disease risk:
🧪 Pesticide accumulation
Sublethal pesticide exposure — particularly neonicotinoids and fungicides — impairs bee immune function, learning, and navigation. Wax accumulates lipophilic pesticide residues over multiple seasons, creating a chronic background stressor in the hive environment.
🌾 Nutritional stress
Landscape simplification and seasonal monocultures reduce floral diversity. Nutritionally stressed colonies have weakened immune responses, making them more vulnerable to pathogens at any mite load.
🌡️ Climate variability
Extended droughts, irregular flowering, and heat events disrupt the seasonal rhythms that bees and beekeepers rely on. Unpredictable nectar dearths increase robbing pressure and colony stress.
🔬 Emerging pathogens
With Varroa established, previously uncommon viruses — Deformed Wing Virus, Acute Bee Paralysis Virus, Israeli Acute Paralysis Virus — are now circulating at much higher levels. The pathogen landscape has permanently shifted.
💡 Why This Module Exists
Comprehensive hygiene guidance for beekeepers already exists in regulatory documents — but it is scattered, focused on listing rules rather than explaining why they matter, and largely written before Varroa arrived in Australia.

What is missing is a practical, pathogen-centred guide that explains:
• Why different pathogens require completely different hygiene strategies
• When in the season each intervention matters most
• What cheap, field-ready methods actually work — and which don't
• How the beekeeper themselves can be a disease vector without knowing it
This module fills that gap. It is built on peer-reviewed science, government biosecurity guidance, and practical knowledge from experienced apiarists and bee inspectors — presented in a format that works in the field, not just on a desk.

🗺️ Disease Transmission Map
Pathogens live in three different reservoirs — tap each to understand what lives there and how it spreads.
🪵
Comb & Equipment Reservoir
AFB · EFB · Chalkbrood · Wax Moth · SHB · Nosema spores
▾
These pathogens persist long after bees have left. AFB spores survive in comb for decades. EFB (Melissococcus plutonius) also persists in contaminated comb and equipment, though less tenaciously than AFB. The hive structure itself is the reservoir — not the bees.
HIGH persistence
Control strategy:
🔥 Flame sterilise tools   🌀 Ozone stored comb   🚫 Never reuse contaminated comb
⚠️ Notifiable disease: Do not destroy equipment until you have reported to your state agriculture authority and received instructions. Destroying evidence before inspection may be an offence.
🐝
Bee Population Reservoir
EFB · Nosema · Chronic bee paralysis · Tracheal mites
▾
These pathogens move with living bees — through trophallaxis, drifting, and direct contact. EFB (Melissococcus plutonius) is primarily transmitted this way — infected nurse bees pass it to larvae during feeding. Equipment hygiene has some impact for EFB but colony strength and bee health are the primary controls for most pathogens in this reservoir.

Trophallaxis — mouth-to-mouth food sharing between bees. A fundamental colony behaviour used to distribute food, pheromones, and information throughout the hive. Because it involves direct oral contact between many individuals, it is also a highly efficient route for pathogen transfer — Nosema spores, viruses, and bacteria can pass directly from bee to bee during every feeding exchange.

Drifting — when forager bees return to the wrong hive, usually due to wind, disorientation, or hives placed too close together in a row. A drifting bee carries its full pathogen load — including Varroa mites — into the new colony. Apiaries with hives arranged in long parallel rows have significantly higher drift rates than those with varied orientations or landmarks. Drifting is one of the primary mechanisms by which Varroa spreads between colonies in an apiary without any beekeeper intervention.
MEDIUM persistence
Control strategy:
💪 Maintain strong colonies   🌬️ Ensure good ventilation   🔄 Rotate comb regularly   🍯 Good nutrition
💥
Collapsing Colony Reservoir
Varroa · DWV · ABPV · IAPV
▾
The most dangerous reservoir in the Varroa era. Virus titres can increase millions-fold as a colony collapses. One dying colony can distribute hundreds of mites to neighbours through robbing — the "Varroa bomb" effect.
EXPLOSIVE spread
Control strategy:
🧪 Monitor mites every 4 weeks   ⚡ Treat before threshold   🗑️ Remove deadouts immediately   🚫 Prevent robbing during dearth
▸ Virus profiles & Australian status
DWV — Deformed Wing Virus
The most destructive honey bee virus globally when Varroa is present. Vectored directly into bee haemolymph during mite feeding, causing deformed wings, shortened abdomen, and neurological damage. Varroa amplifies viral titres millions-fold in collapsing colonies.
🇦🇺 Australian status: Strong evidence DWV (genotype A and B) is currently absent from Australian colonies — never detected in large-scale national surveys. Its arrival via Varroa is the single greatest emerging viral threat. Active baseline monitoring underway via varroa.org.au.
ABPV — Acute Bee Paralysis Virus
Part of the AKI complex with IAPV and KBV. Causes rapid paralysis and death of adult bees within days. Self-limiting without Varroa — kills bees too quickly to sustain chronic infection — but dangerous when mite populations are very high.
🇦🇺 Australian status: Not detected in large-scale surveys to date (Roberts et al. 2017). Included in varroa.org.au baseline monitoring as Varroa spreads. Considered at risk of emerging once mite populations establish.
IAPV — Israeli Acute Paralysis Virus
AKI complex member. Causes trembling, inability to fly, and rapid adult die-off. First linked to Colony Collapse Disorder in the US. Typically co-occurs with DWV and ABPV in severely collapsing hives overseas.
🇦🇺 Australian status: Confirmed present — detected in a national survey of 1,240 colonies (Roberts et al. 2017), with high read abundance in some samples. No CCD association in absence of Varroa, but behaviour as Varroa spreads is being actively monitored.
🚪
New Arrivals Reservoir
Purchased colonies · Swarms · Transferred apiaries
▾
Every new colony entering your apiary — purchased nucleus, full hive, caught swarm, or taken-over apiary — is an unknown biosecurity risk. It may carry AFB spores, Varroa at any level, EFB, Nosema, or viruses with no visible symptoms. Quarantine is the only reliable way to contain this risk.
PREVENTABLE entry point
Quarantine protocol
📍 Location: Place new arrivals at least 3 metres from your existing hives — ideally on the opposite side of the apiary or a separate site. The quarantine position should have a distinct entrance orientation so bees do not drift into established colonies.
🐝 Caught swarms — Swarms are unknown-origin bees. They may originate from a feral colony carrying AFB, or from a beekeeper's colony that collapsed due to disease. Treat every swarm as high-risk. Place in quarantine, perform an alcohol wash immediately to establish a baseline mite count, and inspect brood carefully at the first opportunity (once comb is drawn). Do not allow swarm frames to contact established colony equipment.
🛒 Purchased nucleus or colony — Request a recent alcohol wash result and inspection record from the vendor before purchase. Reputable sellers will provide this. Even so, quarantine for a minimum of 4–6 weeks and perform your own alcohol wash within the first week. Inspect brood for AFB and EFB symptoms at the first brood inspection.
🏚️ Taken-over apiary — Taking over hives from a retiring or deceased beekeeper is high-risk. Disease management history may be unknown, equipment may be old and contaminated, and AFB spores may be present in comb that shows no current symptoms. Treat all equipment as potentially contaminated. Perform brood inspection and alcohol wash on every hive before moving them to your site. Consider replacing old dark comb progressively.
⚠️ Quarantine minimum: Keep new arrivals isolated for at least 4–6 weeks — one full brood cycle. This allows any latent disease to manifest in new brood before the colony is integrated into your apiary. Do not share tools, frames, or equipment between quarantine and established hives during this period.

📅 Seasonal Risk Calendar
Disease transmission in apiaries is strongly seasonal. Focus effort when it matters most.
Aug–Oct
Spring
🌱 Brood Disease Window
Rapid colony expansion, beekeepers handling many frames. AFB, EFB, Chalkbrood risk elevated.

✅ Alcohol wash every 4 weeks — first check of spring confirms whether mites survived winter and whether any treatment is needed before populations build
✅ Record all counts in VarroaMate
✅ Inspect brood carefully
✅ Avoid unnecessary frame exchange between hives
✅ Flame tools between apiaries
✅ Sterilise used equipment before returning to service
✅ Begin comb rotation — move old dark brood frames outward toward the edge of the brood nest; mark any new frames with month and year (e.g. "Sep 25") on the top bar
Nov–Feb
Summer
☀️ Peak Activity — Maintain Monitoring
Good conditions but mite populations building silently. Nectar dearth later in summer increases robbing and drifting risk.

✅ Alcohol wash every 4 weeks
✅ Record all counts in VarroaMate
✅ Reduce entrances if robbing observed
✅ Avoid open feeding or exposed honey frames
Mar–May
Autumn
🚨 Most Critical Window of the Year
Brood declines, mite populations peak, virus loads increase. Colony contraction increases robbing risk.

🚨 Critical Varroa Treatment Window
Winter bees — the long-lived bees that must carry the colony through to spring — are produced from March to April. Any bee that emerges while mite levels are high will have a shortened lifespan and carry heavy viral loads. Treatment must be completed before the end of April to protect this cohort. Missing this window significantly reduces the colony's chance of surviving winter.
🚨 Monitor mites first — alcohol wash to confirm threshold (≥3/300)
🚨 Treat for Varroa immediately if at or above threshold
🚨 Complete treatment before end of April
🚨 Remove deadouts within 24 hours
🚨 Prevent robbing cascades — reduce entrances
May–Aug
Winter
❄️ Equipment Hygiene Window
Low colony activity — lowest transmission risk. Best time for equipment maintenance and forward planning.

✅ Avoid opening the hive — breaking the cluster costs bees energy
✅ Clean and sterilise all equipment
✅ Ozone or freeze stored comb
✅ Inspect and repair hive boxes
✅ Plan next season's Varroa treatment schedule in the VarroaMate Smart Planner — set your treatment rotation and mode-of-action sequence before spring arrives; record your monitoring results in VarroaMate as counts come in
🦠 Eight Bee Health Threats
Most colony losses involve only a handful of biological threats. Understand these and you understand most apiary health problems.
Transmission Pathways
Ranked by frequency of disease spread in Australian apiaries
🐝
Bee-to-Bee
Highest risk · hardest to prevent
#1
Drifting, robbing, and swarming spread Varroa, DWV, Nosema, and EFB directly between colonies. A single robbing event can transfer thousands of mites within hours. Strongest colonies are most at risk of attracting robbers.
Varroa DWV Nosema EFB
🧑‍🌾
Beekeeper
High risk · fully preventable
#2
Moving frames, splitting colonies, and handling bees across hives without hygiene protocols. AFB spores travel on unsterilised hive tools in seconds. The beekeeper is the most efficient disease vector in the apiary — and the most controllable.
AFB Varroa EFB
🔧
Equipment
Medium risk · easily controlled
#3
Second-hand hive boxes, frames, and comb are the primary reservoir for AFB spores, which survive 50+ years in wax. Borrowed or shared equipment without sterilisation is a common source of introduction to clean apiaries.
AFB Chalkbrood Wax moth
🌿
Environment
Lower risk · partially controllable
#4
Soil, vegetation, and water sources carry chalkbrood spores, wax moth eggs, and small hive beetle adults. Wild colonies and feral swarms in the area are a background reservoir for Varroa and other pathogens. Apiary placement and local colony density matter.
